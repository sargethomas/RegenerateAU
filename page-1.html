TROPICAL RAINFORESTS

Pollution, deforestation, and the disruption to the naturally occurring carbon cycle of the biome are all threatening to the tropical rainforests around the world, because of the unethical practices of the food industry.

Pollution as a result of food wastage and unsustainable food production has a dire effect on the rainforests, as it contaminates the air and water in the biome. Pollution has had a direct correlation with the increased acid rain occurrences in rainforests, which can severely damage the environment, by dissolving nutrients in the soil that are needed by trees to remain healthy. Food wastage that is dumped carelessly can end up in landfills which affects the rainforests negatively. Areas of the rainforest that have been converted to suit agricultural purposes have been poisoned by fertilizers that render nearby bodies of water lifeless and unable to support other ecosystems. The overuse of nitrogen fertilizers has caused ‘dead zones’ in bodies of water, where the oxygen content is too low for it to support any more of life. Rivers and lakes nearby areas that have been turned into mining plants have been found to have toxic levels of mercury.

Deforestation is the process of knocking down trees and demolishing forests. The most common reason for deforestation is agricultural practices, the most prominent of the two being palm oil production and cattle ranching. The palm oil industry is notorious for child labour and slave exploitation, but despite this, still provides for 35% of the world’s vegetable oil demand. Deforestation threatens the habitats of many animals like orangutans, pygmy elephants and Sumatran rhinos. As of 2008, cattle ranching accounts for 80% of deforestation in Amazon countries. And considering the impressive co2 emissions of cattle, removing the natural carbon sinks (trees) and replacing them with polluters greatly decreases the air quality around the forests

Deforestation due to cattle ranching causes 340 million tonnes of carbon dioxide to be released into the air per annum. These great emissions, paired with the lack of trees to absorb the carbon, results in the predicament that we are now facing - the biggest rainforest in the world, the Amazon, now produces more carbon dioxide than it absorbs. This along with the higher risk of fires tied into desertification (a consequence of unsustainable farming practices), means that currently, the carbon emissions of the rainforest are likely to rise, and not decrease.
