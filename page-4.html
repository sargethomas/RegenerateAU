ICE CAPS

Rising temperatures, pollution are the biggest problems for this ecosystem, and threaten its integrity and the lives of the organisms that are part of it.

The carbon emissions and rising temperatures (8% of which is constituted by food wastage) pose one of many threats to the biome. As food rots and decays, it produces methane, adding to unneeded atmospheric greenhouse gases. It contains carbon, large amounts of which, can damage the planet severely and cause a spike in the natural temperatures of the ice caps. So far, the melting ice caps from Antarctica alone are adding a little under half a millimetre per annum. Globally, an average of 750 billion tons of ice melts per year, which amounts to oceans rising at a rate of about 3.6mm per year.

Water pollution is another problem that the ice caps face, the most common pollutants being food scraps and waste, plastic containers, and fertilizers used to cultivate crops. At least 8 million tonnes of plastic ends up in the oceans each year, with 80% of all marine debris being waste plastic. A lot of this plastic comes from food production and selling. The wastage of food means there is a lot more plastic produced along with the food, filling rubbish dumps with increasing amounts of non-biodegradable waste. When visiting the ice caps, tourists tend to leave behind rubbish and other waste that can pollute the land and also get washed into the sea, endangering animals. A lot of produce that doesn’t even manage to reach the shelves, meaning there is a lot of waste in the production, marketing, packaging and transportation of that product which may not even be sold (which once again causes plastic pollution and excess litter which can pollute land and water).
