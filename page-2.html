CORAL REEFS

Ocean acidification, and water pollution, and how it affects sick coral are all consequences faced by the coral reef at the hands of the food industry.

Ocean acidification is the process of the ocean’s pH lowering over time due to the absorption of carbon. This is a negative process as marine life cannot survive in highly acidic environments. Fish in coral reefs are already starting to experience the repercussions of ocean acidification - their skeletons become heavier, making them less mobile, and making it almost impossible for them to migrate to safety, it stunts their growth, and their ability to intake oxygen. Coral is extremely sensitive and will react poorly to even the slightest change in water quality or pH. Since 1850, one third and a half of the world’s carbon emissions (25% of which is contributed by the food industry) have been absorbed by the ocean, in turn, raising its acidity by 26% in the nearly 200 years. If the current carbon emission levels are kept up as they have been for the past 100 years, humans will soon experience the full-on effects of ocean acidification, as it will limit the seafood supply, which will put thousands out of work, and many more will suffer, as seafood is a significant source of protein to over 3 billion people

Another problem is the pollution that the ocean faces at the hands of the food industry. 14.5 million tonnes of plastic food containers are produced annually, around 50% of this going to the ocean. Coral is much more likely (89% more likely) to contract a disease while the environment around it is polluted by plastic. Plastic litter can also negatively impact the creatures that often live in or around the reef, like sea turtles, dolphins, sharks, and fish. Commercial fishing for food products also poses a large threat to the reef, as floating or discarded fishing nets can become entangled in hard coral, and catch on parts of the reef and break small branches off, or scratch the coral, as well as tangle into marine life, cutting into arteries, strangling them, or weighing them down, and preventing them from swimming to the surface to breathe
